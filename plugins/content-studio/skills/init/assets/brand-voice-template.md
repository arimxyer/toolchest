# Brand Voice Guide

> Starter template — replace every section with your own content. The `/content-studio:*` skills read this file before drafting, critiquing, or generating headlines.

## About the brand

One paragraph: what the company/site does, who it serves, the core promise. Skills use this to ground every piece in what the brand is actually selling — without it, drafts drift into generic SaaS prose.

- **Product / service:** _e.g. an API for parsing legal contracts_
- **Audience:** _e.g. in-house counsel and contract ops leads at mid-market companies_
- **Mission / promise:** _e.g. cut review time without losing nuance_

## Voice

Three to five adjectives, each with one sentence of nuance. Adjectives alone are useless — the nuance is what makes them actionable.

- **Confident, not boastful.** State what the product does. Don't claim superlatives we can't prove.
- **Precise.** Specific numbers, specific verbs. Cut "powerful," "robust," "enterprise-grade."
- **Plain-spoken.** Conversational rhythm, but not casual. No "let's dive in" or "buckle up."

## Tone shifts by context

Voice is consistent; tone flexes. Specify how:

| Context | Tone |
|---------|------|
| Product announcement | Energetic, but anchored in concrete outcomes |
| Technical deep-dive | Patient, instructional, assumes a smart reader |
| Support / docs | Warm and direct — name the problem, then solve it |
| Customer story | Quiet pride; let the customer be the protagonist |

## Style rules

- **Person:** First-person plural ("we") for brand voice. Second-person ("you") when addressing the reader.
- **Sentence length:** Mix. Mostly 12–20 words. Use short sentences for emphasis.
- **Headings:** Sentence case. No clickbait. A heading should describe what the section says, not tease it.
- **Lists:** Use sparingly. If three items would read better as prose, use prose.
- **Numbers:** Spell out one through nine; numerals for 10+. Always numerals for measurements and percentages.
- **Oxford comma:** _yes / no_

## Vocabulary

### Preferred terms

| Use | Not |
|-----|-----|
| _customer_ | _user_ |
| _document_ | _doc_ (informal) |
| _review_ | _audit_ (legal-charged) |

### Banned / avoid

- _"AI-powered"_ — overused; describe what the AI actually does instead
- _"seamless"_ — vague; show the seam being removed
- _"unlock," "supercharge," "revolutionize"_ — marketing filler
- _Acronyms before first use is defined_

## Audience

One paragraph per primary audience. Include: their job, what they care about, what they already know, what they're skeptical of.

- **Primary:** _e.g. in-house counsel — busy, skeptical of legal-tech vendors, fluent in legal jargon, skim before they read_
- **Secondary:** _e.g. legal ops leads — process-focused, evaluating tools across the team_

## Examples

Pairs are the single highest-signal part of this file. Skills compare a draft to these to catch voice drift.

### On-voice

> Contract review used to take three hours per agreement. With Lexcheck, the first pass takes twelve minutes — and flags the clauses your team would have caught anyway.

Why this works: specific numbers, concrete verb, ends on a reader-validating observation rather than a brag.

### Off-voice

> Lexcheck's powerful AI-driven platform unlocks unprecedented efficiency for legal teams, seamlessly transforming the contract review experience.

Why this fails: stacked filler adjectives, no concrete outcome, "seamlessly" and "unlocks" are banned, no specific reader benefit.

## Article structure defaults

When the brief doesn't specify, default to:

1. **Hook (1–2 sentences)** — state the reader's problem in their language.
2. **Why this matters now** — the change in context that makes this worth reading today.
3. **The core argument or how-to** — the body. Headings every 250–400 words.
4. **What this looks like in practice** — concrete example, screenshot, code sample, or customer quote.
5. **Call to action** — single ask, never more.
